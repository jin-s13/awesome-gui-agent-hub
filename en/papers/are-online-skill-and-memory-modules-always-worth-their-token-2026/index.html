Are Online Skill and Memory Modules Always Worth Their Tokens? A Budget-Constrained Study of Web Agents

arXiv 2026 51.7 method

TLDR

Online web agents' skill and memory modules often fail to outperform a token-matched vanilla baseline under fixed inference budget.

Reasoning

The paper provides a clear, budget-constrained comparison across multiple models and domains, showing that augmentation modules do not consistently beat a vanilla baseline. However, it is limited to specific benchmarks and does not deeply analyze run-to-run variance.

Deep Analysis

Innovations

  • Budget-constrained evaluation of online augmentation modules for web agents, accounting for test-time token costs.
  • Token-matched vanilla baseline that uses the same total inference budget for additional actor steps instead of augmentation modules.
  • Demonstration that apparent performance gains from augmentation often vanish when compared to a budget-matched actor.
  • Identification of run-to-run variance as a material factor that should be reported as a core evaluation criterion.

Methodology

The study compares three online augmentation methods (AWM, ASI, ReasoningBank) against a token-matched vanilla baseline that uses the same total inference budget for extra actor steps. Evaluation is conducted across three WebArena domains and three models (Gemini 3 Flash, GPT-5.4-mini, Qwen 3.6-27B), plus WorkArena-L1 with Qwen 3.6-27B, measuring aggregate success rate and total token usage.

Key Results

The vanilla baseline matches or surpasses all three augmentation methods in aggregate success rate while often using fewer total tokens, and a similar trend is observed on WorkArena-L1, indicating that apparent gains from augmentation vanish under a fixed budget.

Limitations

  • Augmentation methods may still provide benefits in specific domains, as noted by the authors.
  • Evaluation is limited to WebArena and WorkArena-L1 environments; generalizability to other tasks is not established.
  • Only three models were tested, so findings may not extend to all model families or scales.
  • Run-to-run variance materially affects outcomes, meaning single-run comparisons may be unreliable.
